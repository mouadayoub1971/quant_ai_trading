The restaurant vs. food delivery analogy for day trading vs. swing trading is excellent—both involve stocks, but require completely different strategies, tools, and risk management. The long vs. short section introduces the asymmetric power of short selling: **"stock prices usually drop much more quickly than they go up. Fear is a more powerful feeling than greed."** This is market physics—stocks can take months to climb 50%, but drop 50% in days during panic. Short sellers who time this correctly can make "astonishing profits" while everyone else bleeds. But (and this is critical for later) shorting also carries theoretically unlimited risk since stocks can rise infinitely while only falling to zero.

## Detailed Breakdown

### 1. Day Trading vs. Swing Trading: Two Different Businesses

**Andrew's Framework:**

**Day Trading:**
- No overnight positions
- Close all trades by market close (4:00 PM ET)
- Can trade anything, including near-bankrupt companies
- Don't care what happens after market closes
- Time horizon: seconds to minutes, rarely hours

**Swing Trading:**
- Hold positions from one day to a few weeks
- Must select fundamentally solid companies
- Care deeply about overnight risk
- Cannot trade companies that might "lose their entire value overnight"
- Time horizon: days to weeks

**The Restaurant vs. Food Delivery Analogy:**

Andrew uses brilliant analogy:
- **Day Trading = Restaurant**: Fixed hours, serve customers on-premises, immediate transactions, close at night
- **Swing Trading = Food Delivery**: Extended hours, different customer base, different logistics, different revenue model
- **Both involve food (stocks), but completely different operations**

**Critical Distinction:**
- **"You shouldn't use the strategies and tools that you use for day trading to do swing trading."**
- **"They operate with different time frames, regulations, market segments and revenue models."**

**Can You Do Both?**

Andrew does both:
- **"Many traders, including myself, do both day trading and swing trading."**
- **"We are aware that we are running two different businesses"**
- **"We have gone through separate educational programs for these two kinds of trading."**
- **"I do not swing trade and day trade the same stocks."**

**Senior Trader Analysis:**

This is absolutely correct and critically important. The mistakes I've seen:

**Mistake #1: Using Day Trading Techniques for Swing Trades**
- Trader learns ABCD pattern for day trading
- Decides to swing trade using same pattern
- Doesn't account for overnight gaps
- Pattern fails spectacularly
- Account damaged

**Mistake #2: Using Swing Trading Techniques for Day Trades**
- Trader learns fundamental analysis for swing trading
- Tries to day trade based on P/E ratios and earnings
- Ignores intraday momentum and tape reading
- Misses all the fast moves
- Gets chopped up in range-bound stocks

**Mistake #3: Mixing The Two Without Clear Separation**
- Has one account for both day and swing trades
- Unclear which positions are which
- Day trade goes bad, impulsively decides it's now a swing trade
- Swing trade setup appears, but uses day trading position sizing
- Complete mess, impossible to analyze performance

**The Right Approach (If Doing Both):**

**Separate Accounts (Ideal):**
- Account 1: Day trading only ($25k+ for PDT)
- Account 2: Swing trading only (can be smaller, no PDT restriction if <4 day trades/week)
- Never mix the strategies

**Or Separate Mental Accounting (Minimum):**
- Same account, but strict labeling
- "This is a day trade" vs. "This is a swing trade"
- Different position sizing rules for each
- Different stop-loss rules for each
- Review performance separately

**2025 Perspective:**

The rise of algorithmic trading has made this distinction even more important:
- **Day trading** now requires adapting to HFT dynamics, millisecond price changes, instant news reactions
- **Swing trading** now requires understanding social media sentiment, options flow, retail coordination (Reddit/Discord)

These are increasingly specialized skills. The jack-of-all-trades trader who does everything is master of nothing.

### 2. Rule #3: Never Hold Day Trades Overnight

**The Rule:**

**"Rule 3: Day traders do not hold positions overnight. If necessary, you must sell with a loss to make sure you do not hold onto any stock overnight."**

**Andrew's Personal Experience:**

- **"I myself have turned some of my day trades into swing trades, and I paid a heavy price for that."**
- **"Often, many of the stocks we day trade will lose even more of their value overnight."**

**Why Traders Break This Rule:**

- Don't want to accept small loss
- **"Suddenly decide to stay in the trade and hold it overnight, in the 'hope' that perhaps a stock will come back the next day."**
- **"It's a common human inclination to accept profits quickly but to also want to wait until losing trades return to even."**

**The Email Question Andrew Gets:**

**"Several traders over the years have emailed me about this rule, and wondered why I advise them to close their position at the end of the day, even with a loss?"**

**Andrew's Answer:**
- **"Of course I do not want you to lose money"**
- **"But I often see traders suddenly change their plan at the end of the day because they do not want to accept a small loss."**
- **"As a day trader, you must stick to your daily plans."**
- **"You should never change a day trade that was supposed to close at the end of the day into a swing trade."**

**Senior Trader Deep Dive:**

This is **THE rule** that separates professionals from amateurs. Let me show you the exact psychological pattern that destroys accounts:

**The Death Sequence (Real Example Pattern):**

**3:45 PM:**
- Trader long 500 shares XYZ at $10.00
- Currently at $9.60
- Down $200
- Stop loss should have been hit at $9.80 (100 loss), but trader moved it

**3:55 PM:**
- XYZ at $9.55
- Down $225
- Trader thinking: "I've been watching this all day, it's going to bounce"
- Thinking: "If I sell now, I lock in the loss"
- Thinking: "It might gap up overnight on good news"
- **Decision: HOLD overnight**

**Next Morning, 9:35 AM:**
- XYZ gaps down to $8.50 on bad news
- Now down $750 (instead of $200)
- Trader in shock
- Thinking: "I can't sell now, that's 3x worse than yesterday"
- **Decision: HOLD longer**

**10:30 AM:**
- XYZ at $7.80
- Down $1,100
- Trader desperate
- Starts to "average down" (buys more to lower cost basis)
- Buys 500 more shares at $7.80
- Now 1,000 shares, average cost $8.90

**11:00 AM:**
- XYZ at $7.20
- Total position down $1,700
- 6.8% of $25k account
- Trader paralyzed
- Can't sell (too much pain)
- Can't buy more (out of buying power)

**12:00 PM:**
- XYZ at $6.50
- Down $2,400
- **9.6% account drawdown on ONE position**
- Finally forced to sell (margin call or emotional breakdown)

**What Should Have Happened:**

**3:59 PM (Day 1):**
- XYZ at $9.55
- Down $225
- **SELL ALL 500 shares**
- Take -$225 loss
- Account at $24,775
- Move on to next day

**Cost of Breaking Rule #3:**
- **Should have lost: $225**
- **Actually lost: $2,400**
- **Difference: $2,175** (9.7x worse)

**I've seen this pattern HUNDREDS of times.** The loss multiplier from holding overnight varies, but it's typically **3x-10x** worse than just taking the end-of-day loss.

**Why Day Trading Stocks Are Especially Dangerous Overnight:**

The stocks we day trade have specific characteristics:
- **High relative volume** (unusual activity, often news-driven)
- **High volatility** (big intraday swings)
- **Small float** (easier to move, less stability)
- **Weak fundamentals** (often near bankruptcy, as Andrew mentions)
- **News-driven** (gap risk on any headline)

These are **NOT** stocks you want to hold overnight. They're built for intraday momentum, not overnight safety.

**Real Examples (2020-2025):**

**Hertz (HTZ) - May 2020:**
- Day trading at $2-6 range during bankruptcy proceedings
- Great intraday volatility
- Some traders held overnight
- Company announced dilution plan
- Gapped down 40% overnight
- **Holding day trade overnight = account destruction**

**GameStop (GME) - January 2021:**
- BOTH directions were lethal
- Day traders long at $350 who held overnight
- Gapped down to $200 next morning (-43%)
- **OR** day traders short at $100 who held overnight
- Gapped up to $250 next morning (-150% loss - wiped out)

**2025 Reality:** With after-hours trading becoming more active and social media driving overnight volatility, the overnight gap risk is HIGHER now than when Andrew wrote this in 2020.

**The Psychological Fix:**

**Before Market Open:**
- Declare to yourself: "All positions close by 3:59 PM, no exceptions"
- Set calendar alert for 3:45 PM
- Have exit plan ready for every position

**At 3:45 PM:**
- Review all positions
- Calculate what loss will be if exiting at current price
- **Accept it emotionally BEFORE clicking sell**
- Remember: Small loss today > catastrophic loss tomorrow

**At 3:59 PM:**
- Close everything
- Even if it hurts
- Even if "it might bounce"
- Even if "just five more minutes"
- **CLOSE EVERYTHING**

**At 4:01 PM:**
- Positions = ZERO
- Cash = 100%
- Sleep well
- No overnight gap risk
- No stress
- Professional day trader

**The Only Exception (Very Rare):**

If you're running BOTH day trading and swing trading as separate businesses:
- You can hold swing positions overnight
- **BUT:** These must be established as swing trades from entry
- Different position sizing (smaller for swing)
- Different stops (wider for swing)
- Different stocks (fundamentally solid for swing)
- **NEVER convert losing day trade into swing trade**

### 3. Trading vs. Investing: Different Skills, Different Careers

**Andrew's Friends Ask:**
- **"Andrew, you are a trader, can you teach me how to trade too?"**

**What Andrew Discovers:**
- **"Most of them want to invest their money, they are not looking for a new or additional career as a trader."**
- **"They actually want to invest their money themselves rather than settle for the gains that typical mutual funds offer."**
- **"They are not looking to become a trader."**
- **"They don't realize the differences and are incorrectly using the words investing and trading interchangeably."**

**Common Questions Andrew Gets:**
- **"Andrew, do you see the market up or down from here until the end of the year?"**
- **"Apple is selling off, is it a good buy now? Do you think it will go higher?"**

**Andrew's Answer:**
- **"I have no idea."**

**Andrew's Explanation:**

**What He's NOT:**
- **"I am not an investor."**
- **"I do not study long-term trends nor am I trained as an investor."**
- **"I have never developed a long-term investing strategy."**
- **"I am not sure where the overall market is headed in six months or where for that matter Apple will trade even tomorrow."**

**What He IS:**
- **"I am a trader."**
- **"My business is called trading, not investing."**
- **"I do not care where AAPL will trade in two years."**
- **"As a day trader, I am trained for short-term intraday trading, nothing more."**
- **"I am only interested in what stocks will move the most today."**
- **"How I can make money today is my obsession and my expertise."**

**The AAPL Example:**

**If AAPL is a Stock in Play (Chapter 4 preview):**
- **If weak: "I am shorting it"**
- **If strong: "I am long"**
- Personal opinion irrelevant
- Trade what the market gives you TODAY

**The Key Line:**

**"You are a day trader, your time span is measured in seconds and minutes, rarely in hours, and certainly not in days or weeks or months."**

**Senior Trader Analysis:**

This distinction is **profound** and most beginners completely miss it. Let me break down the fundamental differences:

**INVESTOR Mindset:**
- **Question:** "Is this a good company?"
- **Analysis:** Fundamentals (P/E ratio, revenue growth, competitive moat, management quality)
- **Timeframe:** Years to decades
- **Risk:** Company goes bankrupt (rare for blue chips)
- **Skill:** Financial statement analysis, industry knowledge, patience
- **Success Rate:** 60-70% can achieve market returns with index funds
- **Income Timeline:** Decades (compound growth)

**SWING TRADER Mindset:**
- **Question:** "Is this stock in an uptrend/downtrend?"
- **Analysis:** Mix of fundamentals and technicals (trend lines, moving averages, support/resistance)
- **Timeframe:** Days to weeks
- **Risk:** Trend reversal, overnight gaps
- **Skill:** Technical analysis, risk management, market timing
- **Success Rate:** 30-40% can be consistently profitable
- **Income Timeline:** Months to years

**DAY TRADER Mindset:**
- **Question:** "Is this stock moving RIGHT NOW?"
- **Analysis:** Pure price action (Level 2, Time & Sales, volume, tape reading)
- **Timeframe:** Seconds to minutes
- **Risk:** Intraday reversal, getting trapped in position
- **Skill:** Pattern recognition, execution speed, emotional control
- **Success Rate:** 10-16% can be consistently profitable (per earlier 84% failure rate)
- **Income Timeline:** 6-12 months to profitability

**Why "I Have No Idea" Is The RIGHT Answer:**

When someone asks: **"Will Apple go up or down in the next 6 months?"**

**Investor Response:** "Based on their Q4 earnings, Vision Pro sales trajectory, and expanding services revenue, I believe they'll trade higher. I'm buying shares at current levels for 3-5 year hold."

**Day Trader Response:** **"I have no idea, and I don't need to know. Tell me if it's moving up or down RIGHT NOW and I'll trade it accordingly."**

**Neither response is wrong—they're answers to different questions:**
- Investor answers: "Where will this be long-term?"
- Day trader answers: "Where is this going in the next 5 minutes?"

**The Confusion That Destroys Accounts:**

**Beginner Mistake Pattern:**
1. Learns day trading (this book)
2. Opens trading account
3. Sees Apple mentioned in news as "good long-term buy"
4. Buys 100 shares Apple for "day trading"
5. Uses wrong position size (too large for day trade)
6. Uses wrong stop (too wide for day trade)
7. Stock goes against them intraday
8. Holds overnight because "Apple is a good company"
9. Now accidentally an investor with day trader position sizing
10. Gets chopped up in volatility, sells at loss

**The person tried to day trade with investor mentality. Doesn't work.**

**Reverse Mistake (Less Common But Equally Bad):**
1. Investor buys Apple for 5-year hold
2. Learns some day trading concepts
3. Sees Apple down 2% one day
4. Panics and sells (treating long-term investment as day trade)
5. Apple rebounds next day
6. Frustration, buys back higher
7. Repeat process, death by 1,000 cuts
8. Turns winning investment into losing trade

**2025 Context Update:**

The line between trading and investing has blurred for many retail participants:

**What's Confusing Now:**
- **Robinhood/Webull** market these platforms as "investing apps" but design encourages day trading behavior
- **Reddit/WallStreetBets** calls buying GME/AMC "investing" but holding periods are days/weeks (actually swing trading)
- **Influencers** teach "buy the dip" (investor language) on highly volatile stocks (day trading candidates)
- **Crypto** has no clear category (24/7 trading, high volatility, but some hold for years)

**How To Stay Clear:**

**Before Every Trade, Ask:**
- "What is my timeframe for THIS position?"
- "Am I day trading this (close today) or swing trading (hold days/weeks) or investing (hold years)?"
- Use different position sizing based on answer
- Use different stop-loss rules based on answer
- **NEVER mix the categories mid-trade**

**Can You Be Both Trader AND Investor?**

**Yes, but keep them COMPLETELY separate:**

**Account 1: Day Trading**
- $25k-50k
- Close all positions daily
- High volatility stocks
- Tight stops
- High win rate needed

**Account 2: Swing Trading (Optional)**
- $10k-25k
- Hold days to weeks
- Trending stocks
- Medium stops
- Medium win rate needed

**Account 3: Long-Term Investing**
- $50k-500k+
- Hold years to decades
- Quality companies or index funds
- No stops (or very wide)
- Buy and hold, low turnover

**Never transfer money between these accounts mid-month.** They're separate businesses with separate P&L.

### 4. Long and Short: Making Money in Both Directions

**Buying Long (Going Long):**

**Definition:**
- **"Day traders buy stocks in the hope that their price will go higher. This is called buying long, or simply long."**
- **"I am long 100 shares AAPL" = bought 100 shares, want to sell higher**
- **"Going long is good when the price is expected to go higher."**

**Simple Enough:** Buy low, sell higher, make profit. This is what most people understand.

**Selling Short (Going Short):**

**Definition:**
- **"Day traders can borrow shares from their broker and sell them, hoping that the price will go lower and that they can then buy those shares back at a lower price and make a profit."**
- **"I am short Apple" = sold Apple shares borrowed from broker, hope price drops**
- **"When the price is going lower, you owe 100 shares to your broker (it probably shows as -100 shares in your account), which means you must return 100 shares of Apple to your broker."**

**The Critical Detail:**
- **"Your broker doesn't want your money; they want their shares back."**

**Andrew's Example:**

**Profitable Short:**
1. Borrow 100 shares Apple from broker
2. Sell them at $100/share (receive $10,000 cash)
3. Price drops to $90/share
4. Buy back 100 shares at $90/share (spend $9,000)
5. Return 100 shares to broker
6. **Profit: $1,000** ($10,000 - $9,000)

**Losing Short:**
1. Borrow 100 shares Apple from broker
2. Sell them at $100/share (receive $10,000 cash)
3. Price RISES to $110/share
4. Still owe broker 100 shares (not money!)
5. Must buy 100 shares at $110/share (spend $11,000)
6. Return 100 shares to broker
7. **Loss: $1,000** ($10,000 - $11,000)

**Why Short Selling Matters:**

**"Short selling is important because stock prices usually drop much more quickly than they go up. Fear is a more powerful feeling than greed. Therefore, short sellers, if they trade right, can make astonishing profits while other traders panic and start to sell off."**

**Senior Trader Deep Dive on Short Selling:**

This is **one of the most powerful tools** in day trading, but also **one of the most misunderstood and dangerous**.

**The Physics of Stock Movement:**

**UP MOVES (Greed-Driven):**
- Gradual climb
- Stepwise progression
- Buyers slowly bid up
- **Example:** Stock goes $50 → $55 over 3 days

**DOWN MOVES (Fear-Driven):**
- Rapid collapse
- Panic selling
- Sellers dump at market
- **Example:** Stock goes $55 → $50 in 30 minutes

**The Ratio:** Down moves are typically **3x-10x faster** than equivalent up moves.

**Why Fear > Greed:**

**Greed (Going Up):**
- Hope for gains
- Can wait patiently
- "It might go higher tomorrow"
- No immediate urgency
- Controlled buying

**Fear (Going Down):**
- Panic about losses
- Must exit NOW
- "Get me out at any price"
- Immediate urgency
- Panicked selling

**Result:** The elevator up, the window down. Stocks take stairs up, elevator down.

**Real Examples:**

**Facebook (META) - Feb 2, 2022:**
- Closed Feb 1 at $323
- Reported earnings after-hours, poor guidance
- Opened Feb 2 at $237 (-26.4% gap down)
- $86/share drop OVERNIGHT
- If held long overnight: catastrophic
- If positioned short pre-earnings: massive win

**Silicon Valley Bank (SIVB) - March 2023:**
- March 8: $267/share
- March 9: Bank run news, $106/share (-60% in ONE day)
- March 10: Trading halted, then $0 (bankruptcy)
- Long holders: wiped out
- Short sellers: generational trade

**The "Astonishing Profits" Reality:**

In 15+ years, my biggest single-day wins have been shorts:

**Typical Long Day Trade:**
- Buy at $50.00
- Sell at $51.50
- +3% gain
- 500 shares = $750 profit
- Took 2 hours

**Typical Short Day Trade:**
- Short at $50.00
- Cover at $47.50
- -5% drop
- 500 shares = $1,250 profit
- Took 20 minutes

**The down moves are faster and bigger.** That's market reality.

**The Dark Side of Shorting (Critical Warnings):**

**Risk #1: Theoretically Unlimited Loss**
- Long position: Stock goes to $0, lose 100% (capped loss)
- Short position: Stock goes to infinity, lose infinite % (unlimited loss)
- **Real Example:** GameStop January 2021, shorts lost 1,000%+, many accounts wiped out

**Risk #2: Short Squeeze**
- When heavily shorted stock starts rising
- Shorts forced to buy back (cover) to limit losses
- Their buying pushes price higher
- Forces more shorts to cover
- Vicious cycle
- **Price can explode vertically**

**Risk #3: Hard to Borrow / Locate Issues**
- Can't short if broker can't find shares to lend
- Shares might be recalled mid-trade
- Forced to cover at worst possible time

**Risk #4: SSR (Short Sale Restriction)**
- If stock drops 10%+ from previous close
- Can only short on upticks (price going higher)
- Limits short entry opportunities

**Risk #5: Emotional Challenge**
- Shorting feels "unnatural" to beginners
- Betting against companies
- Fighting FOMO when stock rises
- Requires different psychology

**How Professional Day Traders Use Shorts:**

**Use Case #1: Market Down, SPY Red**
- Overall market selling off
- Find weakest stocks (biggest losers)
- Short bounces to VWAP or resistance
- Cover when momentum exhausted
- **This is our "bread and butter" on red days**

**Use Case #2: Parabolic Blow-Off Tops**
- Stock runs from $10 → $15 in minutes (50% move)
- Unsustainable momentum
- Wait for first sign of exhaustion (top tick rejection)
- Short the breakdown
- Cover quickly (these can bounce violently)

**Use Case #3: Failed Breakouts**
- Stock tries to break $50 resistance
- Fails, reverses down
- Short the rejection
- Cover at support levels
- High win rate setup

**What Beginners Get Wrong About Shorting:**

**Mistake #1: Shorting Uptrends**
- "This stock is overvalued, I'm shorting it"
- Stock in strong uptrend
- Gets destroyed fighting the trend
- **Never short a stock just because you think it's "too high"**

**Mistake #2: Holding Shorts Overnight**
- Shorts are EVEN MORE dangerous to hold overnight than longs
- Stocks can gap up 50%+ on buyout news, short seller wiped out
- **Rule #3 applies DOUBLE to shorts**

**Mistake #3: Oversizing Short Positions**
- Forgets about unlimited loss potential
- Uses same size as long trades
- One short squeeze, account blown
- **Shorts should be SMALLER position size than longs (50-75%)**

**Mistake #4: Shorting Low Float Gappers**
- Stock gapped up 100% pre-market on low float
- "It's gotta come down, I'm shorting"
- Low float = easy to squeeze
- Reddit/Discord coordination can squeeze intentionally
- **Account destroyed**

**The Right Way to Learn Shorting:**

**Month 1-3 (Simulation):**
- Practice shorts in simulator
- Learn the different feel (backwards from longs)
- Notice how fast down moves are
- Practice covering quickly

**Month 4-6 (Small Real Money):**
- Start with 100-200 share shorts (very small)
- Only short when SPY clearly red
- Only short stocks already in downtrend
- Cover quickly, don't wait for perfect exit

**Month 7-12 (Building Confidence):**
- Increase size to 300-500 shares
- More aggressive short entries
- Learn to hold through small bounces
- Develop feel for exhaustion vs. reversal

**Year 2+ (Advanced):**
- Full-size shorts (still smaller than longs)
- Short both red and green days (when setup appropriate)
- Understand SSR timing and use it
- Master the quick cover on failed setup

**2025 Short Selling Environment:**

**Harder Now:**
- Retail coordination (Reddit can trigger squeezes intentionally)
- Social media amplification (one viral tweet can spike stock)
- Retail "ape" mentality (buy and hold to hurt shorts)
- Options market (gamma squeezes more common)

**Easier Now:**
- Better tools (real-time short interest data)
- Better locate (more shares available to borrow)
- Lower commissions (can take quick scalp profits)
- More education (understanding of setup improves)

**My 2025 Recommendation:**

**Shorting is ESSENTIAL for day trading success.** On red market days (SPY down), shorts provide 60-70% of profit opportunities. If you can't short, you can only trade 30-40% of available setups.

**BUT:** Start small, learn slowly, respect the risk. One bad short can wipe out weeks of gains if you're not careful.

## How to Apply This in Real Trading

### Immediate Actions (Before Next Trading Day)

1. **Clarify Your Trading Style:**
   - Write down: "I am a day trader" OR "I am a swing trader" OR "I am an investor"
   - If multiple, create separate accounts or mental accounting
   - Define rules for each style separately

2. **Set 3:45 PM Alert:**
   - Phone alarm, calendar reminder, or trading platform alert
   - Label: "PREPARE TO CLOSE ALL POSITIONS"
   - This becomes daily routine

3. **Create End-of-Day Protocol:**
   ```
   3:45 PM Checklist:
   [ ] Review all open positions
   [ ] Calculate current P&L on each
   [ ] Accept that you will close ALL by 3:59 PM
   [ ] Prepare exit orders
   [ ] Breathe, let go of "hoping"

   3:55 PM Checklist:
   [ ] Start closing positions (don't wait until 3:59)
   [ ] Close winners first (lock in profits)
   [ ] Close losers second (accept loss)
   [ ] All positions = ZERO by 3:59 PM
   ```

4. **Write Down Rule #3:**
   - Print or write: **"Day traders do not hold positions overnight. If necessary, I will sell with a loss to make sure I do not hold onto any stock overnight."**
   - Post next to monitor
   - Read aloud daily before market open

5. **Learn Short Selling Mechanics:**
   - Open simulator account if not already
   - Practice shorting 100 shares
   - See how account shows "-100 shares"
   - Practice covering (buying back)
   - Do this 20-30 times until comfortable with mechanics

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**❌ Pitfall #1: "Just this once, I'll hold overnight, the setup is so good"**
- **Why it fails**: Setup is good FOR DAY TRADING, not overnight holding
- **What happens**: Gap against you, lose 3x-10x more than if sold at close
- **Fix**: No exceptions ever. Close at 3:59 PM or don't day trade.

**❌ Pitfall #2: "I'm down $200, if I hold overnight maybe it recovers"**
- **Why it fails**: "Hope" is not a trading strategy
- **What happens**: Wake up down $600, spiral into bigger loss
- **Fix**: Accept small loss today, prevent big loss tomorrow

**❌ Pitfall #3: "I'll call this a swing trade now instead of a day trade"**
- **Why it fails**: Wrong stock selection for swing, wrong position size, wrong stops
- **What happens**: Neither good day trade nor good swing trade, just bad trade
- **Fix**: Decide BEFORE entry if day or swing trade, never change mid-trade

**❌ Pitfall #4: "Shorting is too risky, I'll only go long"**
- **Why it fails**: Eliminates 50% of trading opportunities
- **What happens**: Can't profit on red days, limited income potential
- **Fix**: Learn to short safely with small size, expand skillset

**❌ Pitfall #5: "I'll short this stock because I think it's overvalued"**
- **Why it fails**: Trading on opinion rather than price action
- **What happens**: Stock keeps going up, losses mount
- **Fix**: Only short when price showing weakness on current day, not based on long-term opinion

**❌ Pitfall #6: "I'll use same position size for shorts as longs"**
- **Why it fails**: Shorts have unlimited loss potential
- **What happens**: One short squeeze wipes out account
- **Fix**: Shorts should be 50-75% the size of long positions

**❌ Pitfall #7: "I'll ask my day trader friend where Apple will be next year"**
- **Why it fails**: Wrong person for the question
- **What happens**: Get answer like "I have no idea" and think they're not knowledgeable
- **Fix**: Ask day traders about intraday moves, ask investors about long-term outlook

- Easier to cover quickly (liquidity)

## Glossary

**Day Trading**: Trading style where all positions are closed before market close (4:00 PM ET), no overnight holdings

**Swing Trading**: Trading style where positions are held from one day to a few weeks

**Investing**: Long-term approach where positions are held for months, years, or decades based on fundamental company value

**Rule #3**: "Day traders do not hold positions overnight. If necessary, you must sell with a loss to make sure you do not hold onto any stock overnight."

**Long / Buying Long**: Buying shares hoping price will increase, then selling higher for profit (standard stock purchase)

**Short / Selling Short**: Borrowing shares from broker and selling them, hoping price decreases so you can buy back cheaper and return shares for profit

**Cover / Covering**: Buying back shares that were previously borrowed and sold short in order to return them to the broker

**Stocks in Play**: Stocks with unusual volume/volatility that present day trading opportunities (Chapter 4 will cover in detail)

**Hope Trading**: Holding losing positions "hoping" they recover instead of accepting loss and exiting, usually results in larger losses

**Overnight Risk**: The risk that news or events occurring when market is closed cause stock to gap significantly up or down at next open

**Gap Up/Down**: When stock opens significantly higher or lower than previous close due to overnight news/events

**Short Squeeze**: When heavily shorted stock starts rising, forcing shorts to cover (buy back), which pushes price higher, forcing more covering in vicious cycle

**SSR (Short Sale Restriction)**: Rule triggered when stock falls 10%+ from previous close, allowing shorts only on upticks (price increases)

**Timeframe**: The duration for which a position is intended to be held (day trader: minutes; swing trader: days/weeks; investor: years)

**Position Size**: The number of shares in a trade (e.g., 500 shares = full size, 100 shares = small size)

**Unlimited Loss Potential**: Characteristic of short positions where losses can theoretically be infinite since stock price can rise infinitely

**-100 Shares**: Display showing you owe 100 shares to broker (short position), as opposed to +100 (long position)

**Intraday**: Within a single trading day (intraday trading = day trading)

**Fear > Greed**: Market principle that panic selling happens faster than optimistic buying, causing down moves to be more violent than up moves

**Stock to Zero**: Worst case for long position where company goes bankrupt and shares become worthless (-100% maximum loss)

**3:59 PM Close**: The practice of exiting all day trading positions by 3:59 PM (one minute before market close at 4:00 PM)
